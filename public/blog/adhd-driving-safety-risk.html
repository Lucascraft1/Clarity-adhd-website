ADHD Treatment Is Not Only About Focus. Sometimes It Is About Safety.

Lucas Craft, MMS, PA-C  ·  Published July 6, 2026  ·  Washington State
← Back to blog

ADHD treatment is not only about feeling more focused. Sometimes it is about real-world risk.

One area where this shows up clearly is driving. Adults with ADHD can struggle with attention, impulsivity, distractibility, reaction time, and emotional regulation. Those symptoms do not only affect productivity. They can also affect daily safety.

What the Research Shows

A large 2017 study in JAMA Psychiatry examined motor vehicle crash risk in more than 2.3 million people with ADHD in the United States. Researchers compared crash rates during months when patients were taking ADHD medication versus months when they were not. This was a within-person design that controls for individual differences.

The findings were clinically meaningful. Crash risk was estimated to be about 38% lower in males and 42% lower in females during medicated months compared with their own unmedicated months.

What This Does and Does Not Mean

This does not mean medication eliminates driving risk. It also does not mean medication is the right choice for every person. The study was observational, so it cannot establish that medication alone caused the reduction.

But it does highlight an important point: ADHD treatment should not be framed only around whether someone can sit still, finish paperwork, or be more productive.

ADHD affects functioning. Functioning includes work, school, relationships, routines, and emotional regulation. It can also include safety.

Better Questions for ADHD Care

That is why good ADHD care should ask more than whether symptoms are present. The more useful questions are: Where is ADHD creating impairment in this person's life? What risks are specifically relevant to them? What treatment options have evidence behind them? And what changes are actually showing up in daily functioning?

Driving is one concrete example. But the broader point applies across every domain of a person's life. Impairment is not always visible, and the case for treatment is often larger than the one a patient walks in with.

Reference
Chang Z, Quinn PD, Hur K, et al. Association Between Medication Use for Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder and Risk of Motor Vehicle Crashes. JAMA Psychiatry. 2017;74(6):597 to 603.
